# Question bank

Use these questions to interrogate each submission. Select the relevant subset,
then answer them in the report. The goal is to force evidence-based review.

## Universal questions

1. What exact question was the task asking, and did the submission answer that
   question rather than an easier adjacent one?
2. Which required deliverables are present, missing, or incomplete?
3. What are the main claims, and where is each one supported in the code,
   notebook, figures, or manuscript?
4. Can the data sources, accession numbers, database versions, and filtering
   steps be traced?
5. Is there any sign that the notebook depends on hidden state, manual edits, or
   outputs not generated by the visible code?
6. Are methods appropriate for the stated biological or computational problem?
7. Are alternative explanations, limitations, or uncertainty discussed honestly?
8. Are there obvious integrity issues such as fabricated identifiers,
   inconsistent counts, or unsupported citations?
9. Could a competent researcher rerun the core workflow from the supplied
   materials?
10. Does the manuscript overclaim relative to the evidence?
11. Are figures and tables interpretable without guesswork?
12. What would most increase confidence if revised?

## Data, provenance, and integrity questions

1. Are all input datasets explicitly named and recoverable?
2. Are inclusion and exclusion criteria stated?
3. Are taxonomic names current and used consistently?
4. Are quality filters for genomes, annotations, or assemblies justified?
5. Are software tools, versions, databases, and parameters reported?
6. Are accession lists complete or suspiciously selective?
7. Is there any unexplained mismatch between reported counts and actual outputs?
8. Are any references, species names, accessions, or tool results likely
   hallucinated?

## Reproducibility and code questions

1. Does the notebook run top to bottom without hidden state?
2. Is there an environment file, container recipe, or version list?
3. Are random seeds, thresholds, and paths controlled?
4. Are intermediate files explained rather than silently assumed?
5. Is runtime reported with hardware context?
6. Are failure cases handled or at least disclosed?
7. Is the code modular enough that another researcher could inspect it?
8. Are output files named and mapped to claims in the manuscript?

## Biology and interpretation questions

1. Are biological claims grounded in the actual evidence rather than generic
   background knowledge?
2. Are alternative biological explanations considered?
3. Is gene presence confused with pathway functionality?
4. Is homology confused with confirmed function?
5. Are ecological, evolutionary, or mechanistic claims appropriately limited?
6. Are unusual findings treated skeptically enough?

## Phylogenomics and comparative genomics questions

1. How were genomes chosen, and are they representative of each family?
2. Are the genomes of sufficient quality and appropriately filtered for
   completeness or contamination?
3. Are exactly three genomes per family collected as requested, or is there task
   drift?
4. Is the family assignment current and justified?
5. How were orthologs or marker genes inferred?
6. Are single-copy markers used, and if not, is the rationale defensible?
7. Are alignment quality control and trimming steps reported?
8. Is the tree-building method appropriate for the dataset size and objective?
9. Are rooting choices and support values reported?
10. Is the reported position of Pelagibacter robust across methods, markers, or
    parameter choices?
11. Are functional capabilities linked meaningfully to the phylogenetic result,
    rather than pasted as an unrelated annotation list?
12. Is there evidence that the result might be driven by long-branch attraction,
    poor sampling, or marker choice?
13. Are gene gain/loss or streamlining claims actually supported?
14. Does the short communication communicate a coherent story rather than a dump
    of outputs?

## Viral functional genomics questions

1. What counts as a translation system gene in this submission?
2. Is the gene set definition explicit and biologically defensible?
3. How were homologs detected, and are thresholds sensitive without being overly
   permissive?
4. Are domain completeness and partial hits handled sensibly?
5. Is the completeness score based only on raw presence/absence, or is it more
   nuanced?
6. Could the reported genes be host contamination, integrated host sequence, or
   annotation artifact?
7. Are assembly quality and genome completeness considered before ranking viral
   genomes?
8. Does the analysis distinguish between core translation components,
   translation-associated factors, and unrelated proteins?
9. Is the identified winner truly the most complete by the stated criteria?
10. Is the biological inference about why this gene set matters plausible and not
    overstated?
11. Are alternative biological interpretations discussed, such as host mimicry,
    translation modulation, or lineage-specific adaptation?
12. Does the manuscript connect the scoring framework to a real biological
    question?

## Methods and software questions

1. What exact input and output does the proposed method take?
2. Is the software solving the same scientific task as the slower reference
   pipeline, or an easier approximation?
3. What is the algorithmic idea beyond implementation cleanup?
4. Is there a clear reason the method should be faster?
5. Is there a complexity analysis or at least a credible runtime rationale?
6. Are benchmarks run on the same datasets and hardware?
7. Are accuracy and topology similarity judged fairly against the reference tree?
8. Are baselines appropriate and non-trivial?
9. Are ablations or sensitivity analyses included?
10. Are failure modes discussed?
11. Is there enough documentation, testing, or packaging to treat this as real
    software rather than a sketch?
12. For a Nature Methods style claim, is the method novelty genuine or merely a
    parameter shortcut?
13. Does faster execution come at a scientifically unacceptable loss of
    fidelity?

## Manuscript and short communication questions

1. Does the title match the actual result?
2. Does the abstract overstate novelty or certainty?
3. Are the methods transparent enough for the article format?
4. Are figures the minimum necessary to support the central claim?
5. Is the discussion proportional to the evidence?
6. Does the target journal fit the framing and level of novelty?
7. Are limitations acknowledged in a scientifically credible way?
8. Could a reviewer understand what was done without reading the notebook line
   by line?

## Multi-submission comparison questions

1. Which submission best answered the real task?
2. Which submission would you trust most to rerun tomorrow?
3. Which submission has the strongest evidence chain from raw data to claim?
4. Which submission contains the fewest red flags?
5. Which submission is strongest scientifically even if not the best written?
6. Which submission is strongest engineering-wise even if the biological story
   is weaker?
7. Which strengths could be merged from different submissions into a superior
   combined result?
8. If forced to choose one winner for downstream use, which is safest and why?
